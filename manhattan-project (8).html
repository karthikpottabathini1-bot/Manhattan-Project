01 — Overview

Background &
Origins

The Manhattan Project was a secret U.S.-led program during World War II that developed the first nuclear weapons. Beginning in 1942, it brought together scientists, engineers, and military leaders in a massive, top-secret effort to build a bomb powerful enough to end the war.


Fear drove everything. In 1939, physicists Albert Einstein and Leo Szilard warned President Roosevelt that Nazi Germany might be developing atomic weapons. After Pearl Harbor, the U.S. accelerated research into what became the most consequential scientific program in history.


The formal military structure — the Manhattan Engineer District — was created in 1942. Its name would become synonymous with the entire project.

02 — Chronology

Key Timeline

1938
Nuclear Fission Discovered
German scientists Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann discover nuclear fission, with theoretical explanation by Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch. The atomic age begins in a Berlin lab.
1939
The Einstein-Szilard Letter
Albert Einstein and Leo Szilard write to President Roosevelt warning that Germany may be developing atomic weapons. Roosevelt authorizes uranium research in response.
1942
Manhattan Project Officially Begins
The Manhattan Engineer District is formally established. General Leslie Groves takes command. The first controlled nuclear chain reaction is achieved under Stagg Field in Chicago by Enrico Fermi (Dec. 2).
1943
Los Alamos Opens
J. Robert Oppenheimer establishes the secret weapons laboratory at Los Alamos, New Mexico. Oak Ridge and Hanford sites become fully operational for material production.
Jul 1945
Trinity Test — The First Bomb
July 16, 1945: The first atomic device ("The Gadget") is detonated in New Mexico's Jornada del Muerto desert. The blast equals ~21 kilotons of TNT. Oppenheimer recalls: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds."
Aug 1945
Hiroshima & Nagasaki
Aug. 6 — "Little Boy" dropped on Hiroshima (~70,000 immediate deaths). Aug. 9 — "Fat Man" dropped on Nagasaki (~40,000 immediate deaths). Japan surrenders Aug. 15. WWII ends Sep. 2.
03 — Six Subtopics

The Full
Picture

01
⚠️
Why It Began
Fear that Nazi Germany would develop atomic weapons first drove the U.S. to act. In 1939, Einstein and Szilard warned President Roosevelt in a letter. After Pearl Harbor in 1941, the U.S. accelerated atomic research into a full military program, officially launched in 1942.
↳ Fear of Germany's science changed warfare forever.
02
🎖️
Leadership
General Leslie Groves led military operations. Physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer directed the science at Los Alamos. Together they managed over 130,000 workers — most of whom never knew they were building an atomic bomb.
↳ Science and military merged on an unprecedented scale.
03
📍
Secret Cities
Los Alamos designed the bomb. Oak Ridge enriched uranium. Hanford produced plutonium. Chicago hosted the first chain reaction. Entire communities were built from scratch — addresses were classified, mail was censored.
↳ Entire hidden towns existed for one purpose.
04
⚛️
The Science
Scientists exploited nuclear fission — splitting uranium-235 or plutonium-239 releases massive energy. Two bomb designs emerged: "Little Boy" (gun-type) and "Fat Man" (implosion). The Trinity Test on July 16, 1945 proved it worked.
↳ Physics became the most powerful weapon in history.
05
💥
Hiroshima & Nagasaki
Aug. 6, 1945: Little Boy devastated Hiroshima. Aug. 9: Fat Man destroyed Nagasaki. ~70,000–80,000 died in Hiroshima instantly; ~40,000 in Nagasaki. Total deaths from both cities exceeded 200,000. Japan surrendered Aug. 15, ending WWII.
↳ Two bombs ended a world war — and began an ethical debate.
06
🌍
Global Impact
The Atomic Age began. The U.S. and Soviet Union launched a nuclear arms race that defined the Cold War. The Atomic Energy Commission replaced military control in 1947. Nuclear deterrence became the foundation of modern global strategy.
↳ The Manhattan Project permanently altered geopolitics.
04 — The Weapons

Two Bombs.
One War Ended.

The Manhattan Project produced two distinct atomic weapons, each using a different design and a different fissile material. Both were deployed in August 1945 — three days apart.

Bomb 01 of 02

"Little Boy"
Mark I — Uranium Gun-Type

"Little Boy" was the first atomic bomb ever used in combat. It used a gun-type design — one piece of enriched uranium was fired down a barrel into another, creating a supercritical mass and triggering a nuclear chain reaction. It was dropped on Hiroshima, Japan on August 6, 1945, from a B-29 bomber named the Enola Gay. The explosion instantly killed an estimated 70,000–80,000 people, with total deaths reaching approximately 140,000 by the end of 1945.

Bomb 02 of 02

"Fat Man"
Mark III — Plutonium Implosion

"Fat Man" was a far more complex weapon than Little Boy. Rather than a gun mechanism, it used an implosion design — conventional explosives were arranged precisely around a plutonium core to compress it uniformly inward, triggering a chain reaction. This design had been proven by the Trinity Test on July 16, 1945. It was dropped on Nagasaki, Japan on August 9, 1945. The explosion killed approximately 40,000 people instantly, with total deaths reaching around 70,000 by year's end. Japan surrendered six days later.

05 — Aftermath

Legacy &
Impact

The Manhattan Project did not just end a war — it permanently rewrote the rules of civilization. Nations now possessed the ability to cause extinction-level destruction. The Cold War was born. Military strategy shifted from conquest to deterrence. The entire world learned to live under the shadow of the bomb.

Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds.

— J. Robert Oppenheimer, upon witnessing the Trinity Test, July 16, 1945 — quoting the Bhagavad Gita
Atomic Age
Nuclear weapons became the defining technology of the 20th century. No other invention has so completely shaped geopolitics.
Cold War
The Soviet Union tested its own bomb in 1949, launching a decades-long arms race between two superpowers with enough bombs to destroy the world many times over.
Nuclear Power
Manhattan Project research led directly to civilian nuclear energy, now providing ~10% of global electricity. Nuclear medicine advanced cancer treatment.
Ethics Debate
Scientists like Oppenheimer spent their lives questioning the moral weight of their creation. The debate over the bombings continues to this day.
